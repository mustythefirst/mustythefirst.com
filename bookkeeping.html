The Future of Bookkeeping

There is no doubt about it that accounting software have come a long way. Once upon a time there were 11 steps that was required to do the bookkeeping of a new company:

1. Creating a company

Creating suppliers

Entering purchase invoices

Creating customers

Entering sales invoices

Entering purchase credit notes

Entering sales credit notes

Entering cash/bank payments

Entering cash/bank receipts

Conucting a bank reconciliation

Conducting a VAT return

Now, with the recent development of new accounting software such as Xero and Quickbooks. Using bookkeeping software has become a 6 step process. For example, these days, step 3 and 5 have been combined with step 8 and step 9. Furthermore, today’s accounting software can have business owners connect their accounting software live to their bank accounts. This allows for a bank reconciliation to take place instantaneously. This is another step that has been eliminated and is further to be eliminated from the bookkeeping world.

The 6 steps now required are:

1. Creating a company

2. Creating suppliers

3. Creating customers

4. Entering purchase/ purchases credit notes through the bank account

Completing a VAT return

From those 6 steps, steps 2 and 3 should soon be easily eliminated. Eliminated meaning that a bookkeeper would not have to sit down and manually enter suppliers him or herself. On top of that, step 6 should also be easily eliminated. Once all the sales have been entered correctly with their correct tax codes, why should a VAT return need to be filed manually at the end of the month or quarter. Should the software not just know automatically when to file the correct amount of VAT or can it not be programmed to do that?

This now leaves 3 steps:

1. Creating a company

2. Entering purchase/ purchases credit notes through the bank account

3. Entering sales/ sales credit notes through the bank account

Why is it so difficult to eliminate steps two and three? That is because it requires judgement and human intellect. A computer can not know what is an asset or not for a particular business. For example, a Deliveroo employee that uses a bike for work will have that as an asset under his or her business. However, an accountant who uses his or her bike to get to work can not list the bike under an asset. How would the computer be able to distinguish between the two? Or is it even possible to program a computer to be able to distinguish between the two businesses so it knows for which business the bike is an asset and for which business it is not. In the upcoming years, this just may be possible! With current development in artificial intelligence, it just may be possible. That will then bring bookkeeping down to a process of one step.

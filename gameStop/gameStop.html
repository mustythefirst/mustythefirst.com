Why is GameStop in the News?

As I read the current news about GameStop, I cannot help to not put my two cents in.

Firstly, I will start by explaining what shorting is. Shorting is the process of borrowing shares and selling it immediately; and then buying it back at a lower price. Then pocketing the difference.

For example, say you have a friend at work that has a cheese sandwich, and it costs $10. The same day, a celebrity, Oprah, says that cheese sandwiches are not healthy. You know that this will most likely cause a fall in the price of cheese sandwiches due to anticipated fall in demand. So, you say to your friend “let me borrow your cheese sandwhich and I will give it back to you tomorrow”. As soon as you get the sandwhich, you sell it immediately for $10. Then next morning, as you assumed, based on what Oprah said, cheese sandwiches are now worth $5, so you buy it back and give it back to your friend. You made a profit of $5. Your friend does not care about the price of the cheese sandwhich, they just want it back (share in the company).

Before I go on to talk about GameStop. Let me give you some precedent. In December 2012, the CEO of a hedge fund named Pershing Square, publicly announced that it was shorting the stock of Herbalife, accusing the company of being a pyramid scheme, and that it would be shut down by the authorities. It predicted that its share price will go to zero. Meaning that the fund could buy back the shares at $0. The company was then set to profit $1bn as it shorted one $1bn worth of shares.

However, this caused other hedge fund managers to see an opportunity. That was, to buy the share in huge amounts so the price of it could go up. Some of those hedge fund managers included Carl Icahn and George Soros.

As the share price goes up, the short seller stands to lose because they must eventually buy back the shares that they have borrowed and sold.

Going back to the cheese sandwhich example. Instead of the price going to $5, say it went to $20. You, the borrower, now stands to lose, because you sold at $10 and now must buy back at $20, standing to lose $10. Now, in theory, a share price can go up to infinity. And if it does keep going up, a short seller will not have enough cash or capital to be able to cover their losses. They will then be forced to, by their broker, to close their position and take their losses.

By making the mistake of publicly announcing that Pershing Square were shorting Herbalife stock. Bill Ackman exposed his firm to being ganged up on. And gang up on they did, it is worth noting that Carl Icahn and George Soros were and still are big time investors, with pockets deep enough to be able to squeeze a rival hedge fund.

Because the short seller must buy back the shares they borrowed and sold, the ones ganging up on them stand to make a profit. They would have bought at say $20.00 and the short seller will have to buy back a higher price than that because the sudden increase in demand for the share will cause the price to go higher. The difference will be their profit. Carl Icahn profited more than $1bn and Ackman lost close to $1bn.

This is what is essentially happening with GameStop. Hedge Fund managers made the mistake of showing that they are shorting the stock, because they assume less people will buy CD games and instead buy software versions online. They are predicting that their profits will fall along with GameStop’s share price. Unfortunately for these hedge fund managers, a group of Reddit members decided to rally and pump up the share price of GameStop, causing these hedge funds to be squeezed. If the share price keeps rising, these hedge funds will eventually have to close their positions and take their losses.

Once again, in this case, those buying the stock stand to make a profit, and those that are shorting stand to make a loss. Remember that a share price can rise to infinity, so the higher it keeps rising and the more investors join in the rally, the more that those shorting the stock stand to lose.

What is surprising about this is that hedge fund managers are crying foul, even though this has been something that hedge funds have been doing for decades. Is it fair that Robinhood, an online brokerage firm can limit number of shares that investors can buy in GameStop now? Is that not against their clients best interest and instead in the best interest of the hedge fund managers shorting the stock? What does Robinhood have to gain by limiting the number of shares that their investors can buy in GameStop? Did Carl Icahn and George Soros not due the same thing? yet not have limits placed on the number of share that they could have bought in Herbalife?

I would like to finish on the nature of short selling, as some people wrongly believe it to be a scam. So, should it be illegal? The short answer is no. Short selling, it can be argued, helps prevent volatile price increases and helps reduce the number of financial bubbles. Short sellers eventually help the economy grow steadily over time, to grow at the optimum level. Without short sellers, the stock market will be too risky to invest in. Financial bubbles that burst will wipe out savings. People would not really have a retirement to look forward to as they could not put their money into investments that would get them good returns, if not, the best returns (the stock market). To Imagine what short selling is like for the stock market and the economy. Imagine it as a two-party government. One party constantly holding the other part accountable. Which in return, results in increased progress overtime.
